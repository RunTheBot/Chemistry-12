\section{Introduction}

Density is a physical property that describes the relationship between the mass of a material and its volume. This property may be used to identify a meterial and can be use to compare two different materials. Density is the ratio calculated using  $\rho = \frac{m}{V}$, where $\rho$ is the density, $m$ is the mass, and $V$ is the volume. 

This lab will use the displacement of water to determine the volume of the rubber stoppers, which in addition to the mass taken on a mass scale, will then be used to calculate the desnities of two rubber stoppers. This lab will explore two different methods of finding the volume of the rubber stoppers, one for each size, due to the labs constraint of not being able to use a larger graduated cylinder. The smaller rubber stopper will be measured using a graduated cylinder, while the larger rubber stopper will be measured using a beaker and a watch glass, where the change in mass of the watch glass will the be measured to determine the volume.

\section{Analysis}
\subsection{Density of Small Rubber Stopper}

\noindent
\begin{minipage}[t]{0.45\textwidth}
    \begin{align*}
        \text{Volume} &= \text{Final} - \text{Initial} \\
        &= 16.8 \text{ mL} - 15.1 \text{ mL} \\
        &= 1.7 \text{ mL} \\
        &= 1.7 \text{ cm}^3
    \end{align*}
\end{minipage}
\hfill
\begin{minipage}[t]{0.45\textwidth}
    \begin{align*}
        \text{Density} &= \frac{\text{Mass}}{\text{Volume}} \\
        &= \frac{4.33 \text{ g}}{1.7 \text{ cm}^3} \\
        &\approx 2.55 \text{ g/cm}^3\\
        &\approx 2.6 \text{ g/cm}^3
    \end{align*}
\end{minipage}

\subsection{Density of Large Rubber Stopper}

Assuming the density of water is 1 g/mL.\\
\noindent
\begin{minipage}[t]{0.45\textwidth}
    \begin{align*}
        \text{Volume} &= (\text{Final Mass} - \text{Initial Mass}) \times 1 \text{ g/mL}\\
        &= 41.07 \text{ g} - 25.59 \text{ g} \\
        &= 15.48 \text{ g} \\
        &= 15.48 \text{ mL} \\
        &= 15.48 \text{ cm}^3
    \end{align*}
\end{minipage}
\hfill
\begin{minipage}[t]{0.45\textwidth}
    \begin{align*}
        \text{Density} &= \frac{\text{Mass}}{\text{Volume}} \\
        &= \frac{24.25 \text{ g}}{15.48 \text{ cm}^3} \\
        &\approx 1.567 \text{ g/cm}^3\\
    \end{align*}
\end{minipage}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item 
     \begin{align*}
        \text{Percentage Error} &= \left| \frac{\text{Experimental Value} - \text{Theoretical Value}}{\text{Theoretical Value}} \right| \times 100\% \\
        &= \left| \frac{1.567 - 2.55}{2.55} \right| \times 100\% \\
        &\approx 38.5\%
    \end{align*}
    
    $\therefore$ the percentage error for the method from Part 2 is approximately 38.5\%.
    
    \item 
    In order to find the percentage composition of copper and zinc in a brass sample:
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item Take the mass of the brass sample using a mass scale.
        \item The volume of the brass sample would be determined using water displacement in a graduated cylinder.
        \item The density would be calculated using the formula $\rho = \frac{m}{V}$.
        \item The percentage composition of copper and zinc in the brass sample could be determined by using a system of equations with the known densities of pure copper and pure zinc.
    \end{enumerate}
\end{enumerate}
