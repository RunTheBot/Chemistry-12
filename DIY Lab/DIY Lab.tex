\section*{Purpose}
To compare the measured voltage of non-standard vinegar cells using copper as the inert cathode with the theoretical cell potential and compare measured pH to calculated values through the Nernst equation.

\section*{Background}
The experimental cell uses an inert copper strip as the cathode surface, while the actual reduction is hydrogen ion reduction. The anode is either zinc, aluminum or tin, which will undergo oxidation. 

Because vinegar is a weak acetic acid solution, the actual hydrogen ion concentration is much lower than 1.0 M. This means the measured voltage will be lower than the standard SHE-based values, and the Nernst equation can be used to explain the difference.

The Nernst equation is written as:
\[
E_{cell} = E^\circ_{cell} - \frac{0.0592\,\text{V}}{n} \log(Q)
\]

\section*{Analysis}


\noindent
\begin{minipage}[t]{0.48\textwidth}
\subsection*{Step 1: Prediction of Cell Voltages}
To predict the cell potentials ($E_{\text{cell}}$) under non-standard conditions, where the concentration of Hydrogen ions is not $1\text{ M}$, the Nernst equation may be used.

The Nernst equation at $25^\circ\text{C}$ is:
\[ E_{\text{cell}} = E^\circ_{\text{cell}} - \frac{0.0592}{2}\log\left(Q\right) \]
where $Q$ is the reaction quotient, which can be expressed as:
\[ Q = \frac{[\text{M}^{2+}]}{[\text{H}^+]^2} \]
for Zinc and Tin 
and
\[ Q = \frac{[\text{M}^{3+}]}{[\text{H}^+]^3} \]
for Aluminum, where $M$ represents the metal undergoing oxidation.

\subsubsection*{Find concentration of Hydrogen}
Given the measured pH of $2.4$, the reactant concentration is:
\[ [\text{H}^+] = 10^{-\text{pH}} = 10^{-2.4} = 3.98 \times 10^{-3}\text{ M} \]

\subsubsection*{Sample Calculation: Copper-Zinc Cell}
Using $E^\circ_{\text{cell}} = +0.76\text{ V}$ and assuming an initial metal ion concentration of $[\text{Zn}^{2+}] = 1.0 \times 10^{-5}\text{ M}$:
\begin{align*}
    E_{\text{cell}} &= 0.76 - 0.0296\log\left(\frac{1.0 \times 10^{-5}}{(3.98 \times 10^{-3})^2}\right) \\
    &= 0.76 - 0.0296\log(0.6310) \\
    &= 0.76 - 0.0296(-0.200) \\
    &= 0.76 + 0.0059 \\
    &\approx +0.773615\text{ V} \\
    &\approx \mathbf{+0.77\text{ V}}
\end{align*}
\end{minipage}
\hfill
\begin{minipage}[t]{0.48\textwidth}
\subsection*{Step 2: Prediction of pH from Measured Voltage}
Using the measured cell voltage from the experimental trials, the Nernst equation can be rearranged to calculate the pH of the electrolyte solution.

\subsubsection*{Mathematical Derivation}
Starting with the Nernst equation:
\[ E_{\text{cell}} = E^\circ_{\text{cell}} - 0.0296\log[\text{M}^{2+}] + 0.0592\log[\text{H}^+] \]

Sub $\text{pH} = -\log[\text{H}^+]$:
\[ E_{\text{cell}} = E^\circ_{\text{cell}} - 0.0296\log[\text{M}^{2+}] - 0.0592\text{ pH} \]
\[ E_{\text{cell}} - E^\circ_{\text{cell}} + 0.0296\log[\text{M}^{2+}] = -0.0592\text{ pH} \]
\[ \text{pH} = \frac{E^\circ_{\text{cell}} - E_{\text{cell}} - 0.0296\log[\text{M}^{2+}]}{0.0592} \]

\subsubsection*{Sample Calculation: Copper-Zinc System}
Using the experimental value $E_{\text{cell}} = 0.94\text{ V}$ and assuming a trace metal concentration of $[\text{Zn}^{2+}] = 1.0 \times 10^{-10}\text{ M}$:
\begin{align*}
    \text{pH} &= \frac{0.76 - 0.94 - 0.0296\log(1.0 \times 10^{-10})}{0.0592} \\
    &= \frac{0.76 - 0.94 - 0.0296(-10)}{0.0592} \\
    &= \frac{0.76 - 0.94 + 0.296}{0.0592} \\
    &= \frac{0.116}{0.0592} \\
    &\approx \mathbf{1.96}
\end{align*}
\end{minipage}

1. One significant source of error in the lab was the presence of impurities on the metal strips, as suggested by the qualitative observations of the copper strip. This could have affected the measured voltage by introducing additional redox reactions or altering the effective surface area of the electrode, leading to higher or lower voltages than expected. 
Additionally, during the lab, the Vinegar was not replaced between trials, which could have led to changes in the concentration of acetic acid and thus the pH, affecting the voltage measurements for subsequent trials. Furthermore, the metal strips were not cleaned adequately, although washed, they were not sanded, leaving an oxide layer potentially changing results.

2. If there was an opportunity to re-do the lab, I would ensure that the metal strips were thoroughly cleaned and sanded, to eliminate impurities and oxides on the electrodes. Additionally, I would have used fresh vinegar and measured pH each trail. These two changes would eliminate the two largest sources of error.

3. I wanted to make a battery for my DIY lab as I found the topic interesting. Researching what electrolyte to use I remembered the conditions for $\delta E^{\circ}$ had to be under 1M conditions and wondered if the variation in voltage from $\delta E^{\circ}$ could be used to calculate pH. A quick search returned the Nernst equation and I chose the lab there.
